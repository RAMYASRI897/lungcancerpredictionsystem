SYMPTOMS

Persistent Cough

This isn't just a regular cold-related cough—it’s a chronic cough that lingers for weeks or even months. It may start as a dry, irritating cough, or it might produce sputum. If you already have a smoker's cough, it may change in character—becoming deeper, more frequent, or more painful.

Coughing up blood

A condition medically known as hemoptysis. The blood may be bright red or rust-colored and can range from small streaks in the mucus to larger amounts. This symptom is particularly alarming and often prompts immediate medical attention.

Shortness of breath

As tumors grow and start to block air passages or invade lung tissue, shortness of breath can develop. Even minor physical activities, like walking a short distance or climbing stairs.This is because the lungs are either directly obstructed &affected by fluid buildup around them.

Wheezing

It characterized by a high-pitched whistling sound during breathing. It occurs when airways are narrowed or partially blocked, either by the tumor or due to inflammation. This symptom is often mistaken for asthma or bronchitis, especially in individuals with a history of smoking or chronic lung issues.

Unexpected weight loss

Unexpected weight loss is a hallmark of many cancers, including lung cancer. A person may lose a significant amount of weight without changing diet or exercise habits. This happens because cancer can change how the body uses energy and can suppress appetite.

Fatigue

A profound sense of tiredness that doesn't improve with rest. This isn’t ordinary tiredness—it’s a constant, debilitating fatigue that makes daily activities difficult to manage. Cancer-related fatigue is caused by the energy the body uses to fight the disease and by substances the tumor releases.
